### Title:
Why Mobile Apps Are Crushing Websites in Real Estate Investing (And Why That Matters)

### Subtitle:
The shift from desktop to mobile isn't just convenient—it's fundamentally changing how investors find and evaluate property opportunities.

### Introduction:

For decades, real estate was tied to location. You had to be physically present to evaluate property, meet with agents, and negotiate deals.

Then the internet happened.

Suddenly, you could research properties online without leaving your desk. Zillow and Realtor.com became household names. Real estate websites democratized access to property information.

For a while, that was progress.

Today, we're witnessing another transformation—one that's even more profound than the shift from offline to online. Real estate is moving from websites to mobile apps, and the implications are extraordinary.

This isn't just about convenience (though it is convenient). It's about how technology changes our behavior, decision-making, and investment outcomes.

### The Problem With Real Estate Websites

To understand why mobile is winning, let's first acknowledge what's wrong with traditional real estate websites.

**1. Desktop Dependency**

Real estate websites were designed for desktop browsers. The property search interface, map navigation, document reviewing—all optimized for a mouse and keyboard on a 13+ inch screen.

This worked fine in 2010. It works less fine today.

Why? Because most people don't research real estate while sitting at a desk. They research it while:
- Commuting to work (10 minutes of commute time)
- Walking through a neighborhood they're considering
- Waiting for a meeting to start (5 minutes between calls)
- Lying in bed thinking about their financial future (11 PM thoughts)
- Talking with friends about investment opportunities (casual conversation while out)

Websites don't optimize for these moments. Mobile apps do.

**2. Information Overload**

Real estate websites pack features into a single interface:
- Property search filters
- Neighborhood information
- School data
- Crime statistics
- Mortgage calculators
- Agent directories
- Contact forms

Everything is on one page. Everything is important. Nothing stands out.

When everything is important, nothing is.

Most users spend 90 seconds on a real estate website before leaving. Why? Cognitive overload.

**3. Passive Experience**

Websites are passive. You visit. You search. You read. You decide.

The user does all the work.

Apps can be active. They can send you notifications about new properties. They can learn your preferences and proactively suggest opportunities. They can analyze properties in the background and surface the best matches.

**4. Limited Analytics**

Websites track clicks, page views, and user journeys. That's useful data.

But they don't track:
- How long you spent reading about a specific property
- Whether you saved it, shared it, or ignored it
- What time of day you research most intensively
- Whether you're researching solo or discussing with others
- Whether information gaps prevented you from taking action

Apps can track all of this. And they can use these insights to improve the experience in real-time.

**5. Disconnected Experience**

Real estate websites exist in isolation. You research property on Zillow, check schools on GreatSchools, run numbers in a spreadsheet, exchange emails with brokers.

Everything is disconnected. Everything requires context-switching.

Apps can integrate these experiences into a cohesive flow.

### Why Mobile Apps Are Different

**1. Ubiquity**

A website lives in your browser history. You have to remember it exists, navigate to it, and load it.

An app lives on your home screen. It's always there. It's part of your daily routine.

This dramatically changes engagement. Website users might visit once per week. App users might visit once per day.

For real estate investing, that's a game-changer. More exposure leads to better decisions.

**2. Personalization**

Apps know who you are. They have your profile, your preferences, your investment goals.

Websites don't. You're anonymous. You have to re-enter your preferences every time you visit.

With personalization, apps can:
- Surface properties that match your specific goals (not generic "popular" properties)
- Recommend neighborhoods based on your criteria
- Alert you when new opportunities match your profile
- Suggest investment strategies aligned with your risk tolerance

**3. Notifications**

Websites can't reach you. You have to reach them.

Apps can send notifications:
- New property in your target area
- Price drop on a saved property
- Market alert (e.g., "Interest rates hit 5-year low")
- Neighborhood insight (e.g., "This area appreciated 8% YoY")
- Expert recommendation (e.g., "Lawyer suggests this neighborhood")

These notifications keep you engaged and make you react faster than competitors.

**4. Offline Functionality**

Websites require internet connection. You can't browse properties if you're in the subway or in a remote area.

Apps can cache information locally. Download property listings, maps, and data for offline browsing.

This is crucial for real estate. You might discover a property online, remember seeing it offline, and mention it to someone. That mental recall creates decision momentum.

**5. Hardware Integration**

Apps access your phone's hardware:
- GPS/location services
- Camera (for property photos, video tours)
- Photo library
- Contacts
- Calendar

Websites can't access these features (at least not easily).

For real estate, this means:
- Show properties near your current location
- Let you photograph properties and annotate them
- Remind you about property viewings
- Share properties directly with your contacts via their app

**6. Speed**

Apps are faster than websites. They're optimized for your specific device and operating system.

Loading a property listing on a website might take 2-3 seconds.

Loading it on an app might take 200ms.

In our impatient world, speed matters. It's the difference between "this app is smooth" and "this app is sluggish."

For investment decisions, speed affects behavior. Fast apps encourage exploration. Slow websites encourage abandonment.

**7. Deep Learning**

Apps can use machine learning more effectively than websites because they have:
- More data (every user interaction)
- More context (location, time, device)
- More predictive power (what properties you'll like before you see them)

This enables:
- Better recommendations ("Users like you also saved this property")
- Smarter matching ("These 3 properties fit your profile better than the 23 you searched for")
- Risk mitigation ("Properties in this neighborhood have 6% appreciation vs. 2% market average")

**8. Community & Social**

Apps can build community more effectively.

Websites are transactional. You search, find property, leave.

Apps can create ongoing communities:
- Share properties with friends
- Comment on properties you're considering
- See what investors in your demographic are interested in
- Get recommendations from your network

For real estate, community adds value. Shared knowledge about neighborhoods, agent recommendations, and investment lessons learned.

### The Real Estate App Advantage

When you combine all these factors, mobile apps create a fundamentally different experience than websites.

**Real Estate Website Experience:**
1. I'm sitting at my desk thinking about Costa Rica real estate
2. I Google "Costa Rica real estate" or navigate to Zillow
3. I browse 100+ properties
4. I narrow to 10 based on price and location
5. I click on 3-4 to read details
6. I open a spreadsheet and enter data
7. I email an agent asking questions
8. I wait 24 hours for a response
9. I schedule a call and discuss options
10. After all this, I might be ready to move forward

**Total time:** 10-20 hours spread over 2-4 weeks.

**Real Estate App Experience (with AI):**
1. I'm lying in bed thinking about Costa Rica real estate

3. I answer 8 questions about my investment goals

9. I request a video tour and schedule it for tomorrow
10. I'm ready to move forward

**Total time:** 45 minutes.

That's not incrementally better. That's exponentially better.

The market:

For investors:

Mobile real estate apps become not just convenient, but essential.

### The Future is Mobile

Several trends confirm that mobile is the future of real estate:

**1. Demographic Shift**

Younger investors (the future of real estate investment) don't use websites the way boomers do. They use apps. They expect mobile-first experiences.

As wealth transfers to younger generations, their preferred interface (mobile) becomes the market standard.

**2. Technology Integration**

AR and VR are most accessible through mobile. Imagine:
- Walk through a property virtually using your phone
- See neighborhood changes over time through AR overlay
- Review 3D floor plans while standing in the property

These require mobile, not desktop.

**3. Data Quality**

Mobile apps collect better data than websites. They know:
- How long you looked at each property
- Whether you shared it with anyone
- What time you researched (morning commute, evening, weekend)
- Whether you actually visited the property
- What properties you compared

This data creates a feedback loop that improves recommendations.

**4. Real-Time Intelligence**

Mobile enables push notifications about market changes:
- Interest rate alert: "Rates dropped to 4.8%, your purchasing power increased 12%"
- Market alert: "This neighborhood appreciated 2% this quarter"
- Opportunity alert: "New property matches your criteria"
- Competitive alert: "3 people saved this property this week"

Real-time information changes behavior. It prompts action.

Mobile apps integrate with digital wallets, cryptocurrency platforms, and payment systems better than websites.

For international real estate investing, this matters. You might:

- Manage multiple currency holdings
- Track your investment returns alongside other investments

### Implementation: Building Mobile-First for Real Estate

If you're building a real estate platform, the lessons are clear:

**1. Mobile First, Not Mobile Second**

Design for mobile as the primary interface. Desktop is secondary.

This changes UX decisions:
- Simpler navigation (fewer options per screen)
- Thumb-friendly interface (buttons in reachable areas)
- Fast-loading media (images optimized for mobile)
- Vertical-first layout (long scrolls, not wide scrolls)

**2. Leverage Mobile Hardware**

Use GPS, camera, notifications, and contacts. Not because you can, but because they solve real problems.

**3. Enable Offline Usage**

Cache critical data so users can browse when offline. Sync when reconnected.

**4. Implement Smart Personalization**

Learn user preferences and proactively surface opportunities.

**5. Build Community**

Enable sharing, commenting, and peer recommendations. Real estate benefits from community knowledge.

**6. Integrate with Other Services**

Connect with lawyers, accountants, payment providers, and mortgage services. Make the app the hub, not an isolated experience.

### Conclusion

The shift from websites to mobile apps isn't just a user interface change. It's a fundamental transformation in how people discover, evaluate, and purchase real estate.

Mobile apps provide:
- Better accessibility (always with you)
- Smarter personalization (learns your preferences)
- Faster experiences (optimized performance)
- Deeper engagement (notifications, community)
- Enhanced intelligence (data-driven insights)

For investors, especially those investing internationally (where information gaps are largest), mobile apps represent an evolutionary leap forward.

For the Costa Rica real estate market, mobile apps will democratize access and drive a new wave of international investment.

The future of real estate isn't on your desktop. It's in your pocket.

### Title:
The Future of Costa Rica Real Estate Investing: Why the Next Decade is Historic

### Subtitle:
Costa Rica is entering an investment supercycle driven by macro trends, technological innovation, and information democratization.

### Introduction:

Every generation gets one or two investment opportunities where all the pieces align.

Where macro trends, micro economics, information efficiency, and technological enablement converge to create an outsized opportunity for those who see it early.

Costa Rica real estate is one of those opportunities. Right now.

Not in 5 years. Not in 10 years. Now.

This article explains why, and what it means for your investment future.

### The Macro Tailwinds

**Remote Work Revolution**

In 2019, about 5% of the US workforce worked remotely. By 2020, it was 40%. By 2024, it stabilized at 25-30%.

But that's just the beginning.

Globally, remote work adoption is accelerating. Why? Because it works. Productivity metrics show remote workers are more productive. Companies save on real estate costs. Employees get better work-life balance.

The trend is structural, not cyclical. Remote work is here to stay.

What does this have to do with Costa Rica?

Everything.

Remote workers can live anywhere with internet access. They can earn developed-world salaries while living in countries with lower costs.

This drives migration.

Costa Rica has seen digital nomad visa applications increase 300% since 2020. Why? Because:
- Stable political system
- Excellent internet infrastructure
- Low cost of living
- World-class healthcare
- Central America timezone (convenient for US clients)
- Natural beauty and lifestyle

The remote work trend isn't slowing. It's accelerating. As more companies embrace remote work, more digital nomads will migrate to Costa Rica.

This drives real estate demand.

**Retirement Migration**

In 2020, about 1.2 million Americans over 65 were living internationally. By 2025, it's estimated to be 2.1 million.

Why are retirees moving abroad? Primarily:
- Cost of living (healthcare and housing in US is expensive)
- Tax advantages (some countries offer retiree visas with tax benefits)
- Lifestyle (beaches, weather, culture)
- Healthcare (surprisingly, Costa Rica's healthcare system is excellent and affordable)

Costa Rica is one of the top three destinations for American retirees (along with Mexico and Panama).

Every retiree that moves to Costa Rica:
1. Sells their US house (on average: $300K-500K)
2. Either rents in Costa Rica or buys a property
3. If they buy, they contribute $300K-500K+ to the local real estate market

This is significant capital inflow. And it's growing.

**Tourism Boom**

Costa Rica attracts 3.2 million tourists annually. This is one of the highest per-capita tourism rates in Latin America.

Tourism drives economic activity. Hotels need renovation. Restaurants need location. Vacation rentals need properties.

The tourism sector has grown 8% annually for the past 20 years. This trend shows no signs of slowing.

For real estate investors, tourism means:
- Strong rental yields (property rents to tourists at premium rates)
- Diversified income (mix of long-term residents, short-term tourists, seasonal renters)
- Growing infrastructure investment (airports, roads, utilities)
- Economic resilience (tourism provides steady demand)

**Institutional Capital Allocation**

Institutional investors (pension funds, REITs, family offices) are allocating more capital to emerging markets.

Why?

Developed markets (US, Europe, Japan) are mature. Real estate appreciation is 2-4% annually. Rental yields are 2-3%.

Emerging markets offer higher returns. Costa Rica specifically:
- Real estate appreciation: 5-8% annually (vs. 3-4% in US)
- Rental yields: 4-8% annually (vs. 2-3% in US)
- Tax advantages for investors (investment agreement status)
- Political stability relative to other Latin American markets

The first wave of institutional capital is just starting to enter the Costa Rica market. As more sophisticated investors realize the opportunity, capital will accelerate.

This increases property prices and creates appreciation for early investors.

### The Micro Economics

**Limited Housing Supply**

Costa Rica has a housing shortage. The government estimates a deficit of 400,000 housing units.

Why?

Historically, Costa Rica's economy was focused on agriculture and tourism, not real estate development. Construction capacity is limited. Land is protected (much of Costa Rica is national parks or protected areas).

On the demand side:
- Population growing 1.5% annually
- Migration of remote workers (growing 30%+ annually)
- Retiree migration (growing 15%+ annually)
- Urbanization (people moving from rural to urban areas)

Supply is constrained. Demand is growing.

This is the classic condition for appreciation: scarcity + growing demand = rising prices.

**Economic Diversification**

Costa Rica is transitioning from agriculture/tourism to tech/finance.

The government is actively recruiting tech companies. Why? Because tech creates high-paying jobs that generate tax revenue.

Companies like Intel, Google, Amazon, and many others have operations in Costa Rica.

As the economy becomes more tech-focused:
- Salaries increase
- Local purchasing power increases
- Real estate demand increases
- Property prices increase

This economic transformation creates a secular tailwind for real estate values.

**Currency Stability**

Costa Rica's currency (CRC) has been relatively stable over the long term. This reduces currency risk for dollar-denominated investors.

(Note: Currency does fluctuate, especially against dollar. But relative to other Latin American countries, it's stable.)

For international investors, currency stability matters. You don't want your real estate investment undermined by currency depreciation.

Costa Rica's central bank has demonstrated competence managing monetary policy. This builds investor confidence.

### The Information Opportunity

Here's where it gets interesting.

Despite all these favorable macro trends, most international investors know almost nothing about the Costa Rica real estate market.

Why?

**Market Fragmentation**

The market is fragmented across hundreds of local agents, many Spanish-only websites, and scattered information sources.

If you want to invest in US real estate, you have unified platforms: Zillow, Realtor.com, MLS databases. All property data is centralized and standardized.

Costa Rica has none of this. Information is scattered, inconsistent, and hard to access.

**Information Asymmetry**

Because information is scattered, local players have enormous advantage over foreigners.

A local broker knows:
- Which neighborhoods are up-and-coming
- Which developers have good track records
- Which lawyers can be trusted
- What fair prices are in different areas
- Where regulations are ambiguous

Foreigners know none of this. They're at a massive disadvantage.

This information asymmetry creates opportunity. Those who can aggregate, standardize, and analyze information can make better decisions than competitors.

**Language Barrier**

Much of the market information is in Spanish. For English-speaking investors, this creates friction.

Finding a property is one thing. Understanding the legal implications is another. Reading tax documents. Understanding zoning regulations. Negotiating contracts.

All of this requires Spanish fluency or professional translation.

This friction keeps many qualified investors on the sidelines.

**Regulatory Complexity**

Costa Rican property law is different from US law. There are:
- Foreign ownership restrictions (mostly lifted, but still complex)
- Tax treaties to consider
- Investment agreement status (tax benefits if you qualify)
- Currency regulations
- Title registration peculiarities

Understanding these requires local expertise.

Most foreigners don't have this expertise. So they either:
1. Pay expensive lawyers to explain everything (costs $5K-15K per transaction)
2. Make mistakes (costs even more)
3. Never invest (miss out on returns)

### The Technology Opportunity

Here's the key insight:

When you have:
- Macro tailwinds (growing demand)
- Micro economics (limited supply, growing prices)
- Information asymmetry (knowledge gap, opportunity gap)
- Information fragmentation (no unified platform)

...technology that aggregates information creates outsized value.

Consider:

Zillow didn't create the real estate market. It just aggregated information and made it accessible.

Yet Zillow became a multi-billion dollar company.

Why? Because aggregating information in a fragmented market creates network effects and switching costs.

Once you start using Zillow, you don't switch to Realtor.com. Once you know about Zillow, you don't go back to calling individual agents.

The same opportunity exists in Costa Rica.

A platform that:
- Aggregates property listings from multiple sources
- Provides market analytics
- Explains regulatory requirements
- Connects you with trusted professionals
- Uses AI to surface opportunities

...would be enormously valuable.

It would democratize access to information. It would level the playing field between locals and foreigners. It would enable better decisions.

And because real estate transactions are high-value, even small improvements in decision quality create massive returns.

The convergence of macro trends, micro economics, and technology is happening now.

In 5 years:

### The Risk Factors

Of course, all investments carry risk. Costa Rica real estate is no exception:

**Currency Risk**

The CRC can fluctuate against the dollar. If you buy a $400K property and CRC devalues 20%, your local investment is worth $400K but your dollar equivalent is $320K.

Mitigation: Dollar mortgages are available. You can lock in dollar returns.

**Political Risk**

While Costa Rica is politically stable, emerging markets always carry more political risk than developed markets.

Mitigation: Economic incentives align for politicians to maintain stability. Tourism, real estate, and tech are all incentivized sectors.

**Market Risk**

The market could soften. Appreciation could slow. Yields could compress.

Mitigation: Long-term demographics and remote work trends are structural. Short-term volatility is expected. Long-term secular trend is up.

**Regulatory Risk**

The government could change property laws, raise taxes, or restrict foreign ownership.

Mitigation: Property rights are protected by constitution. Changes would require significant political consensus. Most recent regulatory changes have been investor-friendly (not restrictive).

**Liquidity Risk**

Real estate is illiquid. You can't sell in one day. Sales take weeks or months.

Mitigation: Growing demand makes the market increasingly liquid. As international investors enter, turnover increases.

### What's Next

Several developments will shape the Costa Rica real estate market over the next 5-10 years:

**1. Market Consolidation**

Currently fragmented. Will consolidate around 2-3 major platforms (similar to US).

The first platform to achieve critical mass will capture most of the value.

**2. Institutional Presence**

REITs and investment funds will begin targeting Costa Rica.

This will professionalize the market and increase capital availability.

**3. Development Acceleration**

As the market becomes more professional, developers will accelerate construction.

This will increase housing supply, but also increase property values and economic activity.

**4. International Marketing**

More marketing will target remote workers and retirees.

This will accelerate migration and real estate demand.

**5. Fintech Integration**

Digital payment systems, cryptocurrency, and blockchain will enable easier transactions.

This will increase market efficiency and reduce transaction costs.

**6. Data Transparency**

Public records will be digitized. Market data will be standardized.

This will reduce information asymmetry and democratize access.

If you're considering Costa Rica real estate investment, this is the moment.

The market is entering a growth phase driven by structural trends. Information that was previously hoarded is becoming democratized. Technology is enabling better decision-making.

The first investors to enter will have first-mover advantage. They'll get their pick of best properties at best prices.

Costa Rica real estate isn't a get-rich-quick scheme. It's a generational opportunity driven by:

The next decade will likely be historic for Costa Rica real estate.

### Title:
How Artificial Intelligence is Transforming Property Matching (And Making Better Investments Possible)

### Subtitle:
Machine learning algorithms are fundamentally changing how investors discover, evaluate, and select properties—enabling smarter decisions at scale.

### Introduction:

Real estate investing has a fundamental problem: information asymmetry.

The investor has preferences, goals, and constraints. The market has thousands of properties. The gap between what the investor needs and what exists is often massive.

Traditional solution: hire a broker.

Broker listens to your preferences. Shows you 5-10 properties. You pick one.

This process is:
- Time-consuming (weeks or months)
- Expensive (5-8% commission)
- Limited in options (broker shows you what they think you want, not all options)
- Biased (broker is incentivized to sell any property, not the best one)

Artificial intelligence offers a radically different approach: algorithmic matching.

Instead of one broker showing you 5-10 properties, an algorithm can:
- Analyze your preferences across dozens of dimensions
- Compare those preferences to thousands of properties across 47 data points each
- Score each property on investment potential
- Surface the top 20 matches ranked by likelihood of success

All in seconds.

This transforms real estate investing.

### The Limitations of Manual Matching

Before diving into how AI works, let's understand what humans are limited by.

**Limited Bandwidth**

A human broker can hold maybe 3-5 properties in their head at once while comparing.

Can you compare 47 data points across 5 properties? Sure. But it's cognitively taxing.

Now try comparing 47 data points across 100 properties.

It's not possible. Your brain can't process that volume of information simultaneously.

So humans simplify. They use heuristics. They narrow down based on obvious filters (price, location, size).

They miss nuances.

**Subjective Bias**

Humans have preferences. Consciously and unconsciously.

A broker might prefer modern homes over older ones. Or vice versa. This bias affects what they show you.

They might prefer properties they get higher commissions on. Or properties in neighborhoods their friends recommend.

This bias means you're not seeing properties objectively ranked on merit. You're seeing properties subjectively filtered through human preference.

**Limited Data**

A human broker has experience with maybe 100-500 properties over their career.

That's valuable. But it's also limited.

An algorithm can analyze 10,000-100,000 properties. It can see patterns that humans miss.

For example: "Properties in this neighborhood with this architecture style appreciate 8% faster than the neighborhood average."

A human broker might not notice this pattern, even if they've seen 500 properties. An algorithm analyzing 100,000 properties will identify it immediately.

**Emotional Decision-Making**

Humans make emotional real estate decisions. This house "feels right." This neighborhood has good vibes.

Sometimes emotions are right. Sometimes they're wrong.

An algorithm can't feel vibes. But it can evaluate whether neighborhoods with the vibes you like actually appreciate faster than neighborhoods without them.

It can separate emotional preference from rational investment outcomes.

### How AI Property Matching Works

Let's break down how modern AI systems match investors with properties.

**Step 1: Profile Building**

The process starts with understanding the investor.

The algorithm asks questions:
- How much capital do you have?
- What's your investment timeline?
- What's your risk tolerance?
- What are your geographic preferences?
- What property types interest you?
- What's your expected IRR target?
- Are you interested in active management or passive?
- What's your income level/liquidity situation?

These questions create a multidimensional profile.

Instead of describing you as "medium risk investor," the algorithm knows:
- Investment capital: $500K
- Timeline: 5-10 years
- Risk tolerance: Moderate (can handle 15-20% down years)
- Location: Central America (but open to other regions)
- Property types: Residential only
- IRR target: 8-12% annually
- Management style: Delegated (hire property manager)
- Liquidity: Moderate (liquid reserves for emergencies)

**Step 2: Data Collection**

The algorithm needs property data. It pulls from multiple sources:
- Public property listings
- Tax records
- Deed records
- Rental history data
- Neighborhood demographics
- Economic data
- Natural disaster/risk data
- Infrastructure plans

For each property, it aggregates dozens of data points:

FINANCIAL:
- List price
- Price history (5-year trend)
- Days on market
- Price per square foot (vs. neighborhood average)
- Comparable sales analysis
- Estimated rental income (based on similar properties)
- Tax implications
- Insurance costs

PROPERTY:
- Square footage
- Lot size
- Age and condition
- Amenities
- Property type (residential, commercial, mixed)
- Zoning classification

LOCATION:
- Neighborhood appreciation rate

### Limitations of AI Matching

Of course, algorithms aren't perfect:

**1. Garbage In, Garbage Out**

If the input data is bad, the output will be bad.

Mitigation: Use authoritative data sources, continuously verify, flag data quality issues.

**2. Past Performance Doesn't Guarantee Future Results**

The algorithm learns from historical data. But markets change. What worked in 2020 might not work in 2026.

Mitigation: Algorithms should account for market cycle position and adjust weights accordingly.

**3. Black Box Problem**

Some algorithms (especially deep learning) don't explain their decisions.

Why did it score this property 8.7 instead of 7.9?

Mitigation: Use explainable AI. Always explain the score with reasons.

**4. Doesn't Account for Qualitative Factors**

Maybe you love the neighborhood aesthetically. The algorithm doesn't measure aesthetic preference.

Mitigation: Combine algorithmic matching with human judgment. Algorithms suggest. You decide.

**5. Can't Predict Disruption**

If a major employer announces relocation, or infrastructure improves dramatically, the algorithm might not anticipate this.

Mitigation: Include forward-looking data (development plans, zoning changes, employer announcements).
